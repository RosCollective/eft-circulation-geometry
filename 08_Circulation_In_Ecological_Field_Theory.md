# 8. Circulation in Ecological Field Theory

Circulation appears to be one of the ways fields reorganize under changing conditions.

The observations documented in this volume began with four recurring morphologies:

- Propagation
- Redistribution
- Resonance
- Murmuration

These proved stable enough to describe individually.

Over time, however, circulation began appearing in forms that did not belong neatly to any single morphology.

Centers formed, dissolved, and migrated.
Boundaries appeared and unmade themselves.
Pressure accumulated and released through unexpected pathways.
Coherence emerged in diffuse regions.
Direction appeared without being imposed.
Multiple currents interacted while remaining part of a single field.

The field remained recognizable, but its expressions became increasingly variable.
This variability did not weaken the recognition.
It strengthened it.

Circulation appeared less like a mechanism and more like a living process.
The morphologies remained visible, but they rarely appeared in isolation.

Propagation became intertwined with redistribution.
Redistribution altered conditions for resonance.
Resonance contributed to murmuration.
Murmuration opened new pathways for propagation.

The distinctions remained useful.
The field did not respect them.

Observation increasingly suggested that circulation was not a collection of separate forms operating side by side.

Propagation, Redistribution, Resonance, and Murmuration remained useful distinctions, but they rarely appeared in isolation. Each altered the conditions for the others. Boundaries between them became increasingly permeable.

The original recognition remained intact. Integrity continued to appear through distributed responsiveness. Yet the aperture had widened. Circulation appeared less like a set of discrete forms and more like a living ecological process whose expressions shifted with context.

The four forms described in this volume were the first stable landmarks within that process. They made circulation visible. They do not exhaust what may become visible in the future.
