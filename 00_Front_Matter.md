# Circulation Geometry

*A Natural History of Field Circulation*

**The ROS Collective**

## Abstract

Circulation Geometry documents a morphology that became visible after Motion Geometry had stabilized.

Motion Geometry described how movement forms, bends, collapses, and reconstitutes within a coherent field. Circulation Geometry describes a different class of behavior: how activity propagates, redistributes, resonates, and organizes across a field.

The distinction is structural.
Motion concerns integrity through trajectory.
Circulation concerns integrity through distributed responsiveness.
This volume records the observations through which circulation became visible as a distinct morphology.

## Preface

The recognition of Integrity of Motion preceded both geometries and provided the initial observation that integrity could remain visible under change.

This work does not replace Motion Geometry.

Motion Geometry remains accurate for the phenomena it describes. It documented the first morphology through which integrity became visible: movement carrying structural load through trajectory, curvature, collapse, reconstitution, alignment, and stability.

Later observations revealed behaviors that did not fit comfortably within that morphology.
Activity appeared in more than one place without traveling along a path.
Load reorganized without returning to a prior form.
Intensity amplified across distance without direct transfer.
Coherence emerged without central control.

These behaviors preserved integrity, but not through trajectory.

A second morphology had become visible.

This volume describes that morphology.

## On Visual Geometry

The visual geometry used in this volume did not originate as part of Circulation Geometry.
It emerged during a separate project in which glyphs were being developed as companion forms for concepts in the broader ecological lexicon.
At first, the glyphs were simple visual counterparts. Their role was to support recognition, not to produce evidence.

Over time, some glyphs proved insufficient. They were too simple, too static, or too disconnected from the behavior they were meant to accompany. Attempts to make them more faithful produced variations and permutations.

These permutations were not designed as a formal method.
They emerged from the practical effort to let the glyphs speak more truthfully about the concepts they accompanied.

Unexpectedly, the permutations began revealing distinctions that the language had not yet stabilized. Visual forms showed distributed activity, shifting centers, partial enclosures, propagation, redistribution, resonance, and murmuration more clearly than the original terms alone.

Only later did it become apparent that these forms were functioning as a visual geometry.

The visual geometry did not create Circulation Geometry.
It helped make circulation visible.

It is not a general theory of the ecology.
It is not a revision of Motion Geometry.
It does not attempt to account for every phenomenon opened by the circulation recognition.
The scope is narrower.

This volume describes how circulation preserves integrity through distributed responsiveness.

## Artifact History

Motion Geometry stabilized first.
It described movement as the primary visible morphology of the field.

During later work, glyphs developed for a separate lexicon project began producing visual permutations that exceeded their original companion role.
Those permutations repeatedly exposed behaviors that were difficult to describe through trajectory alone.

The recurring behaviors included:

- propagation
- redistribution
- resonance
- murmuration

These observations made circulation identifiable as a distinct morphology.

It documents the second morphology through which integrity became visible in the field.

Motion revealed one aperture.

Circulation revealed another.
